About this week

This is my first project built with D3.js. I started it while working through D3.js in Action by Anne-Marie Dufour and mostly just trying to get used to how D3 works — and how it thinks. The dataset itself is small and the topic fairly random, but that’s intentional. At this stage the point is simply to practice and get comfortable with the library rather than build a big analysis.

The learning curve with D3 is famously steep (which is something you hear from almost everyone who works with it), and I’m definitely experiencing that. There’s a lot of trial and error, things breaking, and slowly figuring out what is actually happening (error codes are not the same as with Python). It’s also tempting to just rely on AI, which is something I did in my previous London housing project. That was partly an experiment to see: can I build a D3 visualisation mostly through ChatGPT prompts, and how far does that actually get me? It’s cool to see something come to life quickly, but it also raises the question of what part of the process is actually exciting and satisfying when you’re learning.

This project is part of my learning during the Data Visualization Society mentorship program and one of the first visualisations I’m building from the book. Right now it’s mostly about practicing, following random interests, and gradually getting a better feel for how D3 works.

She's Been Writing

female authors leading the charts on goodreads

Source: Goodreads — most read books in the UK over the last 12 months.

With International Women’s Day approaching, women dominate the current Goodreads charts, with female authors behind many of the books readers are adding to their shelves right now. The list spans genres, from fast-paced thrillers to expansive fantasy series.

A closer look at the rankings shows a clear romantasy takeover. We can’t help but notice Rebecca Yarros and Sarah J. Maas holding multiple spots at once: proof that readers are fully committed to dragons, fae politics, and emotional damage (the best kind). Maas has spoken candidly about the difficulty of writing these worlds, including on Call Her Daddy, where she connects the intensity of the work to her own therapeutic journey.
